With respect to Roe v University of Waikato Justice Toogood (pulled out of retirement for the occasion because that's what 'independence of the judiciary' means) asserts that they were entitled to refuse to get my thesis out for external examination on the grounds that I submitted it 'too early'. The Tertiary Education Commission of New Zealand defines the enrolment period (amount of work) that is associated with New Zealand University Qualifications, however. That is their job, function, role, purpose, point. They define a 120 point program of study as being comprised of 34 weeks of work (including the examination period). Here is the evidence for this: Enrolment Periods . Justice Toogood was wrong to have asserted that I tried to complete the qualification 'early' or 'under-time'. I tried to submit it so that the customary or standard 3 months of external examination and up to 10 weeks of minor changes (typos and grammar) could be fixed up so that the hardcopy could be lodged in the library at the 34 week mark. That is not 'early'. That is on time. The Universities of New Zealand (also the judiciary) don't appear to know what it is or means to get work done on time. They refuse to allow it.

The University of Waikato offers a 120 point MPhil qualification. That means it has been approved by the TEC as being 'right sized'. The University refuses to allow anybody to complete that qualification, however. But it doesn't stop there. They believe that if the external examiners say anything about changes needing to be made to the substance (e.g., a referene to be added, a section to be moved around) then they are entitled to require the studnet to re-enrol for an additional 6 months and also undergo another external examination period of up to 3 months.
